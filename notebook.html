Freemium → Premium A/B Testing Engine¶

Does the new Lyrics UI lift free-tier → Premium conversion?¶

Business context. Growth wants to ship a redesigned in-app Lyrics UI that surfaces synced lyrics more prominently, on the hypothesis that deeper engagement nudges free users to convert to Premium. Before a global roll-out we run a controlled experiment. This notebook is the full decision pipeline a Business Analytics team would run:

  1. Sample-ratio (SRM) check — is the randomisation trustworthy?
  2. A/A test — does our pipeline produce false positives?
  3. A/B primary metric — conversion lift, significance, confidence interval.
  4. Power / MDE — was the test adequately powered?
  5. Novelty effect — is the early lift real or a curiosity spike?
  6. Simpson's Paradox — why aggregate numbers can lie.
  7. Business impact — ARPU and projected revenue.
  8. Recommendation to leadership.

Data is synthetic (the live Spotify audio/feature endpoints were deprecated in Nov 2024); the methodology is production-grade.

1 · Sample Ratio Mismatch (SRM) check¶

If the realised split deviates from the intended ratio, randomisation may be broken and every downstream result is suspect. We test the control/treatment counts against the intended 50/50 with a chi-square goodness-of-fit test. We want a high p-value here (fail to reject = split is healthy).

In [2]:
ab = main[main.group.isin(['control', 'treatment'])]
counts = ab.group.value_counts()[['control', 'treatment']].values
expected = np.repeat(counts.sum() / 2, 2)
chi2, p_srm = stats.chisquare(counts, expected)
print(f"Counts  control={counts[0]:,}  treatment={counts[1]:,}")
print(f"SRM chi-square = {chi2:.3f},  p = {p_srm:.3f}", 
      "->  no sample-ratio mismatch (OK)" if p_srm > 0.01 else "->  SRM DETECTED")

Counts  control=22,000  treatment=22,000
SRM chi-square = 0.000,  p = 1.000 ->  no sample-ratio mismatch (OK)

2 · A/A test — validating the pipeline¶

Two groups drawn from the identical population (aa_1, aa_2) should show no significant difference. If they did, our instrumentation or test harness would be producing false positives, and we couldn't trust the A/B. We want p > 0.05 here.

In [3]:
aa = main[main.group.isin(['aa_1', 'aa_2'])]
s = aa.groupby('group')['converted'].agg(['sum', 'count'])
z_aa, p_aa = proportions_ztest(s['sum'].values, s['count'].values)
print(f"aa_1 = {s.loc['aa_1','sum']/s.loc['aa_1','count']:.3%}   "
      f"aa_2 = {s.loc['aa_2','sum']/s.loc['aa_2','count']:.3%}")
print(f"A/A z = {z_aa:.2f},  p = {p_aa:.3f}", 
      "->  no false positive, pipeline is trustworthy" if p_aa > 0.05 else "->  PROBLEM")

aa_1 = 6.000%   aa_2 = 6.007%
A/A z = -0.02,  p = 0.981 ->  no false positive, pipeline is trustworthy

3 · A/B primary metric — conversion lift¶

Now the real comparison: control (existing UI) vs treatment (new Lyrics UI). We report the absolute lift in percentage points, the relative lift, a two-proportion z-test, and the 95% confidence interval on the difference.

In [4]:
g = ab.groupby('group')['converted'].agg(['sum', 'count'])
p_c = g.loc['control','sum']/g.loc['control','count']
p_t = g.loc['treatment','sum']/g.loc['treatment','count']
z, pval = proportions_ztest(g.loc[['treatment','control'],'sum'].values,
                            g.loc[['treatment','control'],'count'].values)
se = np.sqrt(p_c*(1-p_c)/g.loc['control','count'] + p_t*(1-p_t)/g.loc['treatment','count'])
ci = (p_t-p_c-1.96*se, p_t-p_c+1.96*se)
print(f"Control conversion  : {p_c:.3%}")
print(f"Treatment conversion: {p_t:.3%}")
print(f"Absolute lift       : +{100*(p_t-p_c):.2f} pp   (95% CI [{100*ci[0]:.2f}, {100*ci[1]:.2f}] pp)")
print(f"Relative lift       : +{100*(p_t/p_c-1):.1f}%")
print(f"z = {z:.2f},  p = {pval:.2e}  ->  statistically significant" if pval<0.05 else "n.s.")

fig, axb = plt.subplots()
axb.bar(['Control','Treatment'], [p_c*100, p_t*100], color=[GREY, GREEN])
axb.errorbar(['Control','Treatment'], [p_c*100, p_t*100],
             yerr=[1.96*np.sqrt(p_c*(1-p_c)/g.loc['control','count'])*100,
                   1.96*np.sqrt(p_t*(1-p_t)/g.loc['treatment','count'])*100],
             fmt='none', ecolor='black', capsize=5)
axb.set_ylabel('Conversion %'); axb.set_title('Free → Premium conversion (95% CI)')
for i,v in enumerate([p_c*100,p_t*100]): axb.text(i, v+0.25, f'{v:.2f}%', ha='center', fontweight='bold')
plt.tight_layout(); plt.savefig('../outputs/ab_primary.png', dpi=130, bbox_inches='tight'); plt.show()

4 · Power & Minimum Detectable Effect (MDE)¶

A significant result still needs context: with our sample size, what is the smallest effect we could reliably detect at 80% power? If our observed lift comfortably exceeds the MDE, the test was well-powered.

In [5]:
n_per = int(g['count'].mean())
h = NormalIndPower().solve_power(effect_size=None, nobs1=n_per, alpha=0.05, power=0.8, alternative='two-sided')
# convert Cohen's h to approx pp around control base
p2 = np.sin(np.arcsin(np.sqrt(p_c)) + h/2)**2
print(f"n/arm = {n_per:,}  |  base = {p_c:.2%}")
print(f"MDE at 80% power ≈ {100*(p2-p_c):.2f} pp")
print(f"Observed lift   = {100*(p_t-p_c):.2f} pp  ->  "
      + ("test was adequately powered" if (p_t-p_c) > (p2-p_c) else "underpowered"))

n/arm = 22,000  |  base = 5.86%
MDE at 80% power ≈ 0.64 pp
Observed lift   = 2.21 pp  ->  test was adequately powered

5 · Novelty effect — is the lift durable?¶

A new UI often gets a curiosity spike that fades. If we measure on day 1 we may over-state the durable effect. We plot the treatment-vs-control gap by assignment day; a decaying curve that settles to a plateau is the novelty signature. We then re-estimate the lift on the stabilised window (day ≥ 8) — the number we should actually plan around.

In [6]:
daily = (main[main.group.isin(['control','treatment'])]
         .groupby(['assignment_day','group'])['converted'].mean().unstack())
daily['gap_pp'] = (daily['treatment'] - daily['control']) * 100

fig, axn = plt.subplots()
axn.plot(daily.index, daily['gap_pp'], color=GREEN, marker='o', ms=3, lw=2)
stab = daily.loc[daily.index >= 8, 'gap_pp'].mean()
axn.axhline(stab, color='black', ls='--', lw=1, label=f'stabilised ≈ {stab:.2f}pp')
axn.axvspan(0.5, 7.5, color='orange', alpha=0.12, label='novelty window (excluded)')
axn.set_xlabel('Assignment day'); axn.set_ylabel('Treatment − control (pp)')
axn.set_title('Daily treatment effect: novelty spike then plateau'); axn.legend()
plt.tight_layout(); plt.savefig('../outputs/ab_novelty.png', dpi=130, bbox_inches='tight'); plt.show()

6 · Simpson's Paradox — why you must segment¶

This uses a separate, deliberately flawed roll-out. In aggregate, treatment appears worse than control. But split by device, treatment wins in both segments. The cause: treatment was over-allocated to low-converting mobile users (an allocation / SRM bug). Lesson: always check SRM and segment the result before believing an aggregate.

8 · Recommendation to Leadership¶

Decision: ROLL OUT the new Lyrics UI — but plan around the stabilised lift, not the launch-week spike.

What we found. The Lyrics UI produces a statistically significant lift in free→Premium conversion (p < 0.001), well above the test's minimum detectable effect, so the result is real and the test was adequately powered. The pipeline is trustworthy: the A/A test was non-significant and there was no sample-ratio mismatch.

The important nuance. The headline ~2.2pp lift is inflated by a novelty spike in the first week. The durable effect, measured after day 8, is ~1.8pp — that is the number to forecast against. Planning on the launch-week figure would over-promise.

Projected impact. At the stabilised lift and treatment ARPU (~$2.6/mo), a global roll-out across the eligible free base (~5M/month) implies on the order of **~90K incremental subscribers and ~$2.8M in incremental annual subscription revenue** — material enough to prioritise the ship, and conservative since it ignores downstream retention and ARPU upgrades.

Guardrails & next steps.

  1. Roll out to 100%, but continue monitoring conversion for 4–6 weeks to confirm the lift holds past the novelty window.
  2. The Simpson's-Paradox case is a reminder: enforce SRM checks and segment-level reporting in our standard experiment template, so a future allocation bug can't hide a bad result behind a good aggregate.
  3. Re-run a holdback in one region to measure the long-run effect on retention and ARPU, not just initial conversion.

Confidence: high on direction, medium on the exact magnitude until the post-novelty period is observed.
